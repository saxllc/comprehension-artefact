One problem.
Two demos.

India's digital infrastructure can capture a tap in 22 languages. It cannot yet tell whether the person understood what they tapped. These two demos attack that gap from two different layers.

Scroll

When a consent screen is signed, a record of permission is written. No record of understanding exists — anywhere.

Fixing that needs two things: a standard way to record comprehension, and a fast, multilingual way to deliver explanations at population scale. Each demo below isolates one of these, so each can be judged on its own terms.

Path A — The Record

Comprehension Artefact

Question it answers: “What does the institution get?”
One eKYC transaction, two records. A DEPA-style consent artefact (permission, machine-readable) paired with a new comprehension artefact (understanding, human-readable) — generated only after a real teach-back check passes.
For: policy, platform and government audiences — anyone who asks what a comprehension standard would look like.

Duplex Voice + Cache Tiers

Question it answers: “Can this run at India scale?”
Voice-first explanation of the same eKYC moment: ask anything aloud, get an instant answer from pre-generated narration chunks (Tier 1 cache), with live model generation (Tier 2) as fallback.
For: engineering and product audiences — anyone who asks about latency, cost and scale.
Path A — The Record

The tap was always captured.
Now comprehension is too.

A simulated loan-app eKYC consent. Toggle between today's flow and the comprehension layer, in English or Hindi, with the checkpoint read aloud by Sarvam's Bulbul voice. Nothing here requires a server-side model at demo time — it cannot fail in a room.

01

Run today's flow first

Mode “Today's flow” → tick the box → tap I Agree. The consent artefact appears on the right. The comprehension slot stays empty: the tap was captured; comprehension was not.

02

Switch the layer on, in Hindi

Mode “With comprehension layer” → हिं → I Agree. A grade-6 explanation appears — play the orange button to hear it. Answer the teach-back question wrong on purpose. Watch it re-explain more simply.

03

Read the two records

Pass the check. Both artefacts appear side by side — open “show machine record” to see the comprehension-artefact/v0.1 schema: language, modality, attempts, probe outcomes, timing.

Why this matters

DPI standardised permission with the consent artefact. This demo shows its missing twin — a verifiable, auditable record that the user understood a consequential action. Fail the check twice and the flow pauses, recommends assisted help, and writes no consent at all. The record is honest in both directions. This is the piece that can become a standard.

Path B — The Delivery

Explanations at the speed of conversation.

A checkpoint only works if the explanation arrives instantly, in the user's language, by voice. These demos show the architecture that makes that affordable at a billion-user scale: pre-generated narration served from a Redis cache (Tier 1), live model generation only on cache miss (Tier 2).

01

Talk to the duplex demo

Open the eKYC duplex page, pick a language, and ask aloud — “kya share hoga?”, “wapas le sakta hoon?”. Speech is transcribed, matched to an intent, and answered from pre-built audio chunks with near-zero wait.

02

Compare the tiers

The tier-compare page runs the same request both ways: Tier 1 cached narration vs Tier 2 live generation. The latency difference on screen IS the business case for preprocessing.

03

Connect it back

Every cached chunk is exactly the kind of explanation Path A's checkpoint needs. Path B is the engine; Path A is what the engine is for.

Why this matters

Comprehension cannot cost a 4-second model call per screen for a billion users. Pre-generating the known journeys × languages × explanations into a cache turns the marginal cost of being understood into roughly the cost of serving a file — and the duplex pattern shows voice Q&A working on top of the same cache.
